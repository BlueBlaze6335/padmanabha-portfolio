I've been building things for a while now.

AI systems that watch television and answer questions about it. Voice conversion pipelines that make one person sound like another in real time. Multi-agent networks that write screenplays. A mathematical DJ that uses graph theory to find the optimal path through a set. A crime documentary pipeline that handles everything from research to final cut.

Most of this lived on my hard drive, in Slack channels, or in conference proceedings that maybe a few hundred people read. That felt wrong — not because I wanted attention, but because I believe in showing your work. The messy middle. The hypothesis that didn't pan out. The architecture decision you'd make differently now.

## Why "Signal in the Noise"

I'm a signal processing researcher by training. My published work is in voice conversion — teaching machines to transform one person's voice into another's while preserving what was said and how it was said. That's literally the job of extracting signal from noise.

But it's also how I think about everything else. Music is signal — I've been playing tabla since I was a kid, and the thing about Indian classical percussion is that the signal isn't just the beat, it's the space between beats. The silence carries as much information as the sound.

Painting is signal. I love Van Gogh and Monet not because they painted what they saw, but because they painted what they *felt* about what they saw. Expressionism is a compression algorithm — you strip away what doesn't matter and amplify what does. Neural networks do the same thing. I'm not sure that's a coincidence.

## What I'll write about here

Some posts will be technical. How the systems I build actually work — the architecture decisions, the training failures, the things I'd do differently. Not to show off, but because I wish more researchers wrote about the process, not just the results.

Some posts will be philosophical. I think a lot about consciousness, about what it means for a machine to "understand" something, about the ethics of building systems that generate art. I don't have answers. But I find the questions worth sitting with.

And some will just be about music, or drumming, or why Bonham's kick drum on "When the Levee Breaks" is the most important sound in rock history. Or why Mohiner Ghoraguli matters more than people think. Or how mixing psytrance at 2am while a diffusion model trains in the background feels like the most natural state of being.

## The site itself

You might have noticed this isn't a normal portfolio. There's a drone playing underneath — a sustained note that changes as you move through sections. Each section is a frequency. The last section is a music studio where you can make something yourself.

I built it this way because I don't believe in separating the technical from the creative. The same brain that designs multi-agent architectures is the brain that needs to hear Danny Carey's polyrhythmic fills to think clearly. The same hands that write PyTorch code are the hands that play tabla. This site is all of that in one place.

If something here resonates with you — a project, a playlist, a sentence — send me a signal. Play a few notes on the piano first if you want. That's what it's there for.

Welcome to the noise. Let's find the signal together.
